# Beyond Correlation: Quantifying Bitcoin's New Role in Financial Markets Through Structural Breaks, Flow Dynamics, and Systemic Risk

## Comparative Analysis of Methodological Approaches to Regime Shifts

The central analytical challenge posed by the introduction of U.S. spot Bitcoin exchange-traded products (ETPs) in January 2024 is determining the nature of the market shift they induced [[106](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/financial-stability-publications/fsr/special/html/ecb.fsrart202505_01~62255f2625.en.html), [163](https://www.scribd.com/document/986239284/ssrn-5182374)]. The user's initial query frames this as a fundamental methodological choice between two distinct approaches: identifying sharp, discrete structural breaks at specific event dates versus tracking the gradual evolution of factor dominance using time-series techniques like rolling regressions. A comprehensive research agenda must evaluate both paths, as they are not mutually exclusive but rather complementary lenses for understanding a complex transformation. The optimal strategy may involve employing both, depending on the specific research question being asked. This section deconstructs the theoretical underpinnings, practical applications, and inherent limitations of each approach within the context of the provided data and recent financial discourse.

The first approach, centered on structural break tests, is designed to detect abrupt changes in the parameters of a statistical model. Econometric tools such as the Chow test, which identifies a single break point, and the more flexible Bai-Perron test, which can identify multiple unknown break points, are particularly suited for investigating the immediate aftermath of a major policy or market event like the ETF approval [[98](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ijfe.70179)]. The underlying hypothesis is that the ETF approval was a "regime change" event, fundamentally altering the laws of motion governing Bitcoin's price dynamics. Proponents of this view might argue that the relationship between Bitcoin and traditional assets, the sensitivity of its volatility to macroeconomic news, or the role of sentiment shocks all underwent a sudden, discontinuous shift. For instance, a study could use a Chow test to compare regression coefficients for Bitcoin returns on a set of macro factors (e.g., S&P 500 returns, VIX, real yields) estimated over a pre-approval period (e.g., Jan 2023 - Dec 2023) versus a post-approval period (e.g., Jan 2024 - present). A statistically significant difference would provide evidence for a structural break. Similarly, a Bai-Perron test applied to the full post-event sample could identify if there were subsequent, discrete events (e.g., specific market crashes or regulatory announcements) that caused further parameter shifts [[98](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ijfe.70179)].

However, the structural break methodology has notable limitations. Its power is contingent on correctly specifying the model and the timing of the potential break. If the change was not instantaneous but rather a gradual process of integration, a structural break test may fail to detect it or may incorrectly pinpoint a break date that is arbitrary. Furthermore, these tests can be sensitive to the chosen estimation window and significance levels, potentially leading to spurious findings [[90](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/wp47.pdf)]. Recent literature highlights that event shocks and structural breaks do exert substantial influence on market efficiency, but the precise timing and nature of these breaks can be difficult to ascertain [[98](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ijfe.70179)]. The European Central Bank, for example, has noted that while the COVID-19 pandemic caused a clear structural break in March 2020, assessing the impact of more nuanced institutional changes like ETFs requires more subtle tools [[43](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/research-publications/working-papers/html/index.sv.html?skey=Survey%20of%20professional%20forecasters)]. Therefore, while powerful for testing a "big bang" hypothesis, this approach may be insufficient for capturing the entire narrative of the post-ETF era, which could involve a prolonged period of evolving investor behavior and market structure.

In contrast, the rolling-window framework offers a continuous-time perspective on how market relationships evolve. By repeatedly estimating a model (e.g., a simple OLS regression or a more complex VAR model) over a moving window of data, researchers can track the time-varying nature of key parameters like factor betas, correlations, and Granger causality. This method is less reliant on pre-specifying a break date and is better suited for identifying gradual, ongoing processes. For example, a rolling regression of Bitcoin returns on S&P 500 returns with a 365-day window would produce a time series of "rolling betas," visually illustrating how Bitcoin's equity risk exposure has changed over time. Recent studies have successfully used rolling correlation analysis to demonstrate that Bitcoin's correlation with the S&P 500 tripled post-ETF approval, providing strong evidence for its increasing integration with equity markets [[83](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/riddhiman-das-967009191_cryptohedgingstrategies-riskmanagementforkids-activity-7440491863385542656-xMVt)]. Similarly, a rolling TVP-VAR model can capture how the impulse response of Bitcoin to a shock in another asset class changes over time [[142](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00181-026-02897-3)]. The primary drawback of this approach is the trade-off between stability and responsiveness. A short rolling window is more responsive to rapid changes but produces noisier estimates, while a long window provides stable estimates but may obscure important, faster-moving dynamics [[139](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/lezz_method-fractal-price-activity-7445686350454034432-8sWt)]. The choice of window length is therefore a critical, and often subjective, decision point in the analysis.

The ideal research program, therefore, would not choose one approach over the other but would instead deploy them in a complementary fashion. A robust study could begin with a structural break test to formally assess the hypothesis that the January 2024 ETF approval constituted a single, significant disruption. A positive finding would lend credence to a narrative of a "new beginning." However, even if a break is detected, the rolling-window analysis remains essential for understanding the path of adjustment *after* the break. It can reveal whether relationships stabilized quickly or continued to evolve in a more complex manner. For example, a Bai-Perron test might identify the ETF approval as a break point, but a rolling beta plot could show that Bitcoin's equity exposure continued to climb gradually over the following months, indicating a prolonged integration process rather than a one-time reset. This dual approach allows for a more complete and nuanced answer to the core research question. It acknowledges both the possibility of discrete, event-driven shifts and the reality of continuous, evolutionary change, which are both likely components of the post-ETF landscape. This methodological synergy is reflected in recent academic work that combines event-study methodologies with dynamic factor models to disentangle the immediate impact of shocks from their longer-term transmission channels [[132](https://www.sec.gov/files/cft-written-sifma-digital-assets-12-16-2025.pdf), [134](https://arxiv.org/html/2602.03874v1)]. Ultimately, the choice of method should be dictated by the specific sub-question being investigated. If the goal is to test a binary hypothesis ("Did the ETF approval change everything?"), a structural break test is appropriate. If the goal is to understand the detailed mechanics of integration ("How exactly did Bitcoin's risk profile evolve after the ETF approval?"), a rolling-window or time-varying parameter framework is superior. A comprehensive research agenda will employ both.

### Proposal 1: Bitcoin's New Normal: An Empirical Investigation of its Evolving Risk Profile and Asset Class Positioning Post-ETF Approval

This paper directly confronts the central debate in modern asset management: Has Bitcoin been permanently reclassified from a speculative digital collectible into a mainstream financial asset? By employing a sophisticated yet interpretable Time-Varying Parameter Vector Autoregression (TVP-VAR) model, this research moves beyond simplistic correlation metrics to provide a dynamic, multi-factor assessment of Bitcoin's risk profile and its relationship with established asset classes. The core contribution lies in its ability to quantify how Bitcoin's conditional betas, its role as a diversifier, and its response to macroeconomic shocks have evolved since the landmark ETF approvals. The findings will be highly relevant for institutional allocators, academics, and policymakers seeking to understand the long-term implications of this market integration.

**Final Proposed Title:** *Bitcoin's New Normal: An Empirical Investigation of its Evolving Risk Profile and Asset Class Positioning Post-ETF Approval*

**Abstract Summary:** This paper investigates how the introduction of U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs in January 2024 reshaped Bitcoin's relationship with traditional financial assets. Using a Time-Varying Parameter Vector Autoregression (TVP-VAR) model with stochastic volatility, we analyze the dynamic evolution of Bitcoin's exposure to equity, bond, and precious metal markets. Our findings indicate a significant increase in Bitcoin's correlation with equities, aligning it more closely with a growth-risk asset class. However, our analysis of its risk-adjusted performance relative to long-duration U.S. Treasuries suggests that its role as a long-term safe haven or diversifier remains ambiguous. The study concludes by quantifying the incremental explanatory power of different factor blocks, providing a nuanced picture of Bitcoin's current place in the global financial landscape.

**Hypotheses:**
*   **H1 (Equity Integration):** Bitcoin's conditional beta to the S&P 500 will exhibit a statistically significant and sustained increase following the January 2024 ETF approval.
*   **H2 (Bond Decoupling):** Bitcoin's conditional beta to long-duration U.S. Treasuries (e.g., TLT) will decrease or remain flat, failing to establish a consistent negative correlation characteristic of a safe-haven asset.
*   **H3 (Gold Fracture):** The dynamic correlation between Bitcoin and gold will not strengthen; instead, it may remain low or even turn negative during periods of macroeconomic stress, confirming a fractured relationship observed in late 2025 [[91](https://www.fintechweekly.com/news/bitcoin-gold-silver-relationship-outlook-2026-year-end-analysis)].
*   **H4 (Performance):** The long-run Sharpe ratio of Bitcoin will be lower than that of a diversified portfolio containing a significant allocation to U.S. Treasuries, challenging its utility as a pure risk-reward enhancer.

**Data Blocks Required:**
*   **Asset Returns:** Daily closing prices/returns for Bitcoin (BTC), the S&P 500 Index, the iShares TLT Bond ETF, and Gold (e.g., GLD or spot price).
*   **Macroeconomic Factors:** Daily data for the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX), the ICE U.S. Dollar Index (DXY), U.S. 10-year Treasury real yields, and a broad corporate credit spread (e.g., Baa-Aaa spread).

**Recommended Sample Design:**
*   **Frequency:** Daily.
*   **Period:** January 1, 2024 – December 31, 2025. This anchors the analysis on the ETF approval and captures a full year of post-event dynamics.
*   **Robustness Window:** An additional analysis covering January 2023 – December 2024 can be performed to check for structural breaks and ensure the 2024 start date is appropriate.

**Baseline Model Family:**
*   A four-variable Time-Varying Parameter Vector Autoregression of order one (TVP-VAR(1) model. The endogenous variables will be the daily log returns of BTC, S&P 500, TLT, and Gold. The model will incorporate stochastic volatility to allow the variance-covariance matrix of the error terms to also change over time, which is crucial given the known volatility clustering in crypto markets [[44](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/375979389_The_Cryptocurrency_Market_Through_the_Scope_of_Volatility_Clustering_and_Leverage_Effects), [78](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/joes.70062)].

**Robustness Model Family:**
*   To complement the TVP-VAR, a Bai-Perron multiple structural break test will be applied to the same dataset to formally test for the presence, number, and location of any significant breaks around the ETF approval date [[98](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ijfe.70179)]. The results from the rolling betas in the TVP-VAR will be compared against the break dates identified by Bai-Perron to see if the gradual evolution seen in the former corresponds to a discrete jump in the latter.

**Acceptable Supporting ML / Big-Data Methods:**
*   While the core analysis relies on classical econometrics, machine learning can be used in an exploratory capacity. Lasso regression could be employed as a variable screening tool to help select the optimal lag length for the macroeconomic factors entering the VAR, reducing model complexity without losing explanatory power [[164](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1568494625016953)].

**Proposed Empirical Workflow:**
1.  **Data Preparation:** Obtain and clean all necessary daily datasets. Merge them onto a common date index, handling missing values through interpolation or by using only fully observed dates.
2.  **Baseline Estimation:** Estimate the baseline TVP-VAR(1) model with stochastic volatility using Bayesian methods (e.g., Markov Chain Monte Carlo).
3.  **Dynamic Analysis:** Extract and plot the time-varying impulse response functions (IRFs) to show how a shock to the S&P 500, VIX, or real yields affects BTC returns over time. Plot the rolling conditional betas for each factor to visualize the changing risk exposures.
4.  **Structural Break Analysis:** Perform the Bai-Perron test to identify potential break points. Compare the break dates with the evolution of the rolling betas.
5.  **Performance Evaluation:** Calculate and compare the historical annualized Sharpe ratios of Bitcoin, a hypothetical portfolio of Bitcoin and Treasuries, and a portfolio of only Treasuries over the full sample period.
6.  **Robustness Checks:** Run static VAR models with and without the ETF approval dummy variable to see if the inclusion of time variation significantly improves the model fit.

**Likely Contribution Relative to Recent Literature:**
*   This paper contributes by moving past the descriptive statistics of rolling correlations [[83](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/riddhiman-das-967009191_cryptohedgingstrategies-riskmanagementforkids-activity-7440491863385542656-xMVt)] and providing a formal, multi-dimensional framework for assessing Bitcoin's asset pricing properties. It builds on works that use VAR models to analyze Bitcoin's relationship with stock markets [[142](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00181-026-02897-3), [144](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/340357391_The_Relationship_Between_Bitcoin_and_Stock_Market)] but adds the critical dimension of time-variation and a richer set of comparative assets (bonds, gold). The focus on risk-adjusted performance relative to Treasuries is a novel angle that directly addresses the practical concerns of portfolio managers.

**What Would Make the Paper Stand Out:**
*   The use of a TVP-VAR model allows for a much richer, more dynamic story than previous static or rolling-window studies. Instead of just saying "correlations changed," it can show *how* and *why* they changed in response to different types of shocks. The visual output—time-varying IRFs and beta plots—is intuitive and compelling for a wide audience. The conclusion regarding Bitcoin's ambiguous safe-haven status, supported by quantitative performance metrics, will be a timely and impactful contribution.

**Why it is Realistic for a Small MSc Team:**
*   The core methodology (TVP-VAR) is a standard topic in advanced econometrics and time-series analysis courses. While computationally intensive, it can be implemented using well-established software packages (e.g., R's `tvvar` package or Python libraries). The data requirements are modest and fall within the scope of typical financial databases. The project's success hinges on solid execution of a known technique to a novel question, which is a hallmark of strong MSc-level research.

### Proposal 2: The ETF Effect: Quantifying the Impact of Institutional Flows on Bitcoin's Price Formation and Volatility

This research project tackles one of the most anticipated questions in crypto finance: What tangible effect do ETF flows have on the underlying asset? This paper aims to provide direct, empirical evidence that institutional inflows via U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs have become a dominant force in Bitcoin's price discovery process and a significant catalyst for market volatility. By linking a concrete, observable phenomenon (daily net flows) to Bitcoin's price action and volatility, this study moves the discourse from abstract speculation to a rigorous, mechanism-based explanation of market dynamics. The findings will be invaluable for traders, asset managers, and regulators seeking to understand the mechanics of the new institutional-era market.

**Final Proposed Title:** *The ETF Effect: Quantifying the Impact of Institutional Flows on Bitcoin's Price Formation and Volatility*

**Abstract Summary:** This paper provides empirical evidence on the role of U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs as a new and powerful driver of Bitcoin's price and volatility. By employing an Augmented Autoregressive Distributed Lag (ARDL) model and variance decomposition from a Vector Autoregression, we find a statistically significant and economically meaningful relationship between ETF net flows and subsequent BTC price movements. Furthermore, our analysis reveals that ETF flows are a substantial contributor to the forecast error variance of Bitcoin's volatility, indicating their role as a catalyst for market turbulence. These findings suggest that ETFs have successfully integrated Bitcoin into the mainstream capital markets, creating a new, transparent channel for institutional participation.

**Hypotheses:**
*   **H1 (Price Impact):** Positive (negative) ETF net flows will have a statistically significant positive (negative) contemporaneous and lagged effect on BTC returns. The impact is expected to be most pronounced in the first few days following the flow announcement.
*   **H2 (Volatility Catalyst):** The variance decomposition of BTC volatility, derived from a VAR model, will show a significant share of the forecast error variance attributable to shocks in ETF flows.
*   **H3 (Flow Sensitivity):** The sensitivity of ETF net flows to changes in the BTC price will be asymmetric, with outflows being larger and more persistent than inflows following a price decline, reflecting the "fallen angel" phenomenon observed in traditional finance [[147](https://cryptoslate.com/bitcoins-next-major-move-hinges-on-a-63-billion-fallen-angel-signal-that-most-investors-are-completely-ignoring/)].

**Data Blocks Required:**
*   **Asset Returns:** Daily closing prices for Bitcoin (BTC).
*   **ETF Flows:** Daily net flow data for the suite of U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs (e.g., BlackRock's IBIT, Fidelity's FBTC). This data is typically reported by providers like Bitwise or publicly on the funds' websites.
*   **Market Conditions:** Daily measures of BTC realized volatility (e.g., 30-day annualized standard deviation of log returns).

**Recommended Sample Design:**
*   **Frequency:** Daily.
*   **Period:** January 1, 2024 – Present (e.g., end of Q1 2026). This captures the entire lifecycle of the ETF market from inception to maturity.
*   **Sub-period Analysis:** A separate analysis focusing on the first 180 days (Jan-Jun 2024) could be conducted to specifically test for any initial "hype" or "pop" effects that may differ from the subsequent stabilization phase.

**Baseline Model Family:**
*   A bivariate Vector Autoregression (VAR) model with two lags (VAR(2)). The endogenous variables will be the daily log return of BTC and the daily net flow of Bitcoin ETFs (scaled appropriately, e.g., as a percentage of market cap). Impulse Response Functions (IRFs) and Forecast Error Variance Decomposition (FEVD) will be generated from this model.

**Robustness Model Family:**
*   An Augmented Autoregressive Distributed Lag (ARDL) model will be used as a robustness check. In this specification, the daily BTC return is the dependent variable, and the independent variables include its own lags, lags of the ETF flows, and lags of other control variables (e.g., S&P 500 returns, VIX). This model is particularly useful for testing for Granger causality and estimating the cumulative effect of flow shocks.

**Acceptable Supporting ML / Big-Data Methods:**
*   Machine learning can be used to enhance the robustness analysis. A Lasso regression can be employed to perform variable selection on the many potential lagged terms of ETF flows and control variables, helping to identify the most predictive lags and avoid overfitting in the ARDL model [[164](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1568494625016953)].

**Proposed Empirical Workflow:**
1.  **Data Alignment:** Clean and merge the daily BTC price/flow data and ETF net flow data. Ensure all data is aligned on a common date basis.
2.  **Baseline Estimation:** Estimate the baseline bivariate VAR(2) model. Conduct diagnostic checks for residual autocorrelation and normality.
3.  **Impulse Response and Variance Decomposition:** Generate and plot the IRFs to trace the response of BTC returns and volatility to a one-standard-deviation shock to ETF flows. Calculate and plot the FEVD to show the percentage of forecast error variance in BTC returns and volatility that is attributable to ETF flow innovations.
4.  **ARDL Specification:** Estimate the ARDL(p,q) model, using information criteria (AIC, BIC) to select the optimal lag orders p and q.
5.  **Asymmetry Test:** Split the sample into periods of net inflows and net outflows to formally test for the asymmetry predicted by H3.
6.  **Robustness Checks:** Include other institutional demand proxies (if available) or macro factors in the VAR/ARDL to ensure the identified effect is specific to ETF flows.

**Likely Contribution Relative to Recent Literature:**

**What Would Make the Paper Stand Out:**

### Proposal 3: Monetary Policy Transmission Through Crypto: The Role of Stablecoins as a Channel to the Digital Economy

This proposal ventures into a frontier area of research, examining the hypothesis that stablecoins—the linchpin of the DeFi ecosystem—are becoming a direct conduit for the transmission of U.S. monetary policy into the digital economy. As major stablecoins hold significant positions in U.S. Treasury bills, a direct transmission channel from monetary policy to DeFi liquidity conditions has emerged [[92](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.03874)]. This paper aims to provide the first robust empirical test of this hypothesis, analyzing the relationship between movements in the U.S. short-end yield curve and the supply of USD-backed stablecoins. If successful, this research would establish a critical, empirically-grounded link between traditional monetary policy and the rapidly growing digital asset economy, a major gap in the literature with profound implications for systemic risk analysis.

**Final Proposed Title:** *Monetary Policy Transmission Through Crypto: The Role of Stablecoins as a Channel to the Digital Economy*

**Abstract Summary:** This paper examines the hypothesis that stablecoins serve as a conduit for monetary policy transmission from the traditional financial system to the decentralized finance (DeFi) ecosystem. Using panel regression and local projection methods, we analyze the relationship between short-term U.S. Treasury yields and the daily supply of major USD-backed stablecoins. Our results provide strong evidence that tightening in the short-end of the U.S. yield curve is associated with a contraction in stablecoin supply, and vice versa. This finding suggests that DeFi is no longer operating in a vacuum and is now subject to the same forces of monetary tightening and easing that affect the broader economy.

**Hypotheses:**
*   **H1 (Supply Elasticity):** A 10-basis-point increase in the 1-month or 3-month U.S. T-bill rate will be associated with a statistically significant decrease in the daily net inflow of stablecoins (i.e., a larger outflow or smaller inflow).
*   **H2 (Regime Dependence):** The magnitude of the effect of yield curve changes on stablecoin supply will be larger during periods of elevated market-wide risk aversion (proxied by the VIX) or credit stress.
*   **H3 (Channel Specificity):** The relationship will be strongest for the short end of the yield curve (e.g., 1-3 months) and weaken for longer maturities, consistent with the investment horizons of stablecoin reserve managers.

**Data Blocks Required:**
*   **Stablecoin Supply:** Daily data on the total supply or circulating token count for the three largest USD-backed stablecoins (USDT, USDC, and ideally DAI). This data can be sourced from on-chain analytics firms like Arkham Intelligence or Chainalysis.
*   **U.S. Treasury Yields:** Daily data on the full U.S. Treasury yield curve, with a particular focus on short-term rates (e.g., 1-month, 3-month, 6-month constant maturity yields).
*   **Control Variables:** Daily data for macroeconomic uncertainty (VIX), credit spreads, and other crypto market indicators (e.g., BTC volatility) to control for confounding factors.

**Recommended Sample Design:**
*   **Frequency:** Daily.
*   **Period:** January 1, 2021 – Present. This extended period is necessary to capture the market dynamics across different monetary policy regimes, including the period of quantitative tightening that began in 2022.
*   **Panel Structure:** The analysis will treat the three stablecoins as entities in a panel dataset, allowing for entity-specific effects.

**Baseline Model Family:**
*   A Fixed-Effects Panel Regression model. The dependent variable will be the daily net change in the total stablecoin supply. The key independent variable will be the change in short-term U.S. Treasury yields. The model will include stablecoin-specific fixed effects, time fixed effects, and a rich set of control variables.

**Robustness Model Family:**
*   Local Projection methods will be used as a semi-parametric alternative to the panel regression. This approach estimates the impulse response of stablecoin supply to a yield shock over a multi-day horizon without imposing a restrictive autoregressive structure, offering greater flexibility [[7](https://www.bis.org/publ/work1270.pdf)].

**Acceptable Supporting ML / Big-Data Methods:**
*   No advanced ML is strictly necessary for the core analysis. However, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) could be applied to the U.S. Treasury yield curve data to extract a single "slope" or "level" factor, which could then be used as the key explanatory variable to reduce multicollinearity.

**Proposed Empirical Workflow:**
1.  **Data Acquisition and Cleaning:** Secure and clean the daily stablecoin supply data and U.S. Treasury yield data. This is the most critical and potentially challenging step.
2.  **Baseline Estimation:** Estimate the baseline fixed-effects panel regression model.
3.  **Local Projections:** Estimate the impulse responses using the local projection methodology to confirm the panel regression results and provide a more flexible dynamic picture.
4.  **Sub-group Analysis:** Stratify the sample based on market regime (e.g., pre/post-QE tapering, high/low VIX periods) to test for the regime-dependency hypothesis (H2).
5.  **Robustness Checks:** Test different specifications, such as using the absolute level of yields instead of the change, or including interaction terms between yield changes and market stress indicators.

**Likely Contribution Relative to Recent Literature:**
*   This paper would fill a significant void in the literature. While the Basel Committee has discussed the prudential treatment of cryptoasset exposures [[102](https://www.bis.org/basel_framework/chapter/SCO/60.htm?inforce=20260101&published=20240717), [103](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/publ/d545.pdf)] and the BIS has highlighted the financial stability risks from cryptoassets [[126](https://www.esrb.europa.eu/pub/pdf/reports/esrb.report202510_cryptoassets.en.pdf), [156](https://www.bis.org/publ/bppdf/bispap138.pdf)], a direct empirical link between monetary policy and the crypto ecosystem has not been established. It builds on work showing that stablecoin flows impact Treasury yields [[7](https://www.bis.org/publ/work1270.pdf)] but flips the causality to test if monetary policy drives stablecoin flows.

**What Would Make the Paper Stand Out:**
*   The paper's contribution is its extreme novelty and high policy relevance. Establishing stablecoins as a monetary policy transmission channel would be a landmark finding, placing the crypto economy squarely within the purview of macroprudential regulation. The visual output—a plot showing stablecoin supply reacting to Fed rate decisions—would be highly intuitive and impactful.

**Why it is Realistic for a Small MSc Team:**
*   The primary hurdle is not the methodology, which is standard econometrics, but the data acquisition. Securing reliable, clean, and comprehensive daily data on stablecoin reserves or supply is non-trivial and may require access to proprietary APIs or paid datasets. Once the data is obtained, the analysis is straightforward. Therefore, this project is rated as moderately realistic, contingent on data availability.
